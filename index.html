Analysis of relationship between GDP and Population


The official definition of GDP is: "Gross domestic product (GDP) is a monetary measure of the market value of all the final goods and services produced and sold (not resold) in a specific time period by countries". We often see GDP serve as an important indicator of a country's economic growth. However, the relationship between total GDP and Population could be controversial, it also leads to another question of whether Total GDP or GDP per Capita is a better indicator of a country's economic development.

The choropleth map above displays the total GDP for each country around the world in the year 2021 where the gradient of the color indicates the difference in total GDP. The darker the color is, the higher the GDP is. When thinking about the total GDP, it is instinctive to think that country with a larger population will have a higher total GDP. However, in the real situation, the relationship between population growth and GDP growth is controversial.

The three graphs above show the top 10 countries ranked by: Population, GDP per Capita, and Total GDP. From the graphs, we can see that the total GDP and Population do not have a direct relationship which means a country with a large population doesn't imply a high total GDP. i.e, the United States has the highest total GDP but doesn't have the largest population.

Vice versa, North America has a relatively small population but has the highest total GDP. Therefore, when using GDP as an index of ecnomic growth, it's worth mentioning the GDP per Capita.

In conclusion, when using GDP as a analysis indicator, it is worth to consider all three factors: Total GDP ,GDP per Capita, and Population. Since there isn't any direct relationship in between those three factors.
